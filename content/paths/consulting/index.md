{{< lead >}}
Management consulting is the classic structured route out of a humanities degree: companies pay firms for clear thinking about hard problems, and the firms hire smart generalists — including philosophers — to supply it.
{{< /lead >}}

The daily work is decomposing a messy question ("why is this division losing money?") into parts, testing each against evidence, and presenting a defensible recommendation — a philosophy tutorial with revenue numbers. Unlike most fields on this site, consulting has a well-marked front door: structured graduate schemes, defined interview formats, and yearly intakes.

## The lay of the land

The industry has tiers: the strategy firms known as **MBB** (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), the **Big Four** (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG — audit and tax at the core, with large consulting arms), and countless **boutiques** specialising in one industry or function. You join as an **analyst** or **associate**, work in small teams on 2–12-week client projects, and the ladder to **engagement manager** and **partner** is explicit. Job titles to search: *business analyst, associate consultant, graduate consultant*. The entry hurdle is the **case interview** — a solve-this-problem-aloud format that rewards exactly the structured, assumption-surfacing thinking philosophers practise; it's learnable, and firms publish prep materials. Consulting is also a common *first* chapter: two or three years in, people exit into strategy roles, startups and policy with a toolkit and a network.
