{{< lead >}}
Venture capital is professional judgement under deep uncertainty: which unlikely-sounding idea is actually right? Two Oxford philosophy graduates in our community run a fund — and the daily work looks surprisingly like what philosophers already do.
{{< /lead >}}

A venture investor's core outputs are arguments: an investment memo is an essay defending a controversial thesis against objections, and diligence is structured skepticism. Philosophers who can hold a strong view, update honestly on evidence, and see why everyone else might be wrong have the raw material for this field.

## The lay of the land

VC firms ("funds") raise money from investors and place it into young companies in exchange for equity. Teams are tiny — often fewer than ten people. The junior rungs are **analyst** and **associate** (finding companies, researching markets, writing memos); **principals** and **partners** make the final calls and sit on boards. Job titles to search: *venture analyst, investment associate, platform associate* (the community-and-support side of a fund). There's no standard entry route — funds hire ex-founders, ex-consultants and sharp generalists — which cuts both ways for a philosopher: no box to tick, but no box to be excluded by either. A realistic first step: write and publish your own investment-style theses; funds notice people who already think in public.
