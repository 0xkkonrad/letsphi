Philosophers start companies. Several founders in our own community — and Let's Phi itself — began with a philosophy degree and a question nobody else was asking properly.
{{< /lead >}}

Founding rewards exactly what philosophy trains: reasoning from first principles instead of copying what exists, holding conviction while updating on brutal feedback, and explaining a new idea clearly enough that people fund it, join it and buy it. Nobody asks a founder for their degree.

## The lay of the land

A startup's life runs in stages: an **idea**, a first version (**pre-seed/seed**, usually 2–10 people), then growth rounds (**Series A** and beyond) as it finds paying users. You don't have to found one to be in one: **early employees** do a bit of everything — operations, community, writing, sales — and job titles are loose (*founding team, chief of staff, operations lead, generalist*). The trade is real: more ownership and learning speed than any graduate scheme, less stability and structure. A realistic first step is either joining an early team to learn how the machine works, or simply starting something small and public — a newsletter, a community, a tool — which is precisely how Let's Phi began.
